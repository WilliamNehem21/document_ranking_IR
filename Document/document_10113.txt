The 1749 restoration at Covent Garden, under the correct title of "Savior", saw the presence of two female soloists who were from now on intently connected with Handel's music: Giulia Frasi and Caterina Galli. In the next year these were joined by the male alto Gaetano Guadagni, for whom Handel made new forms out of "However who may stand" and "Thou craftsmanship gone up on high". The year 1750 additionally observed the establishment of the yearly philanthropy exhibitions of "Savior" at London's Foundling Hospital, which proceeded until Handel's passing and past. The 1754 execution at the emergency clinic is the first for which full subtleties of the instrumental and vocal powers endure. The ensemble included fifteen violins, five violas, three cellos, two twofold basses, four bassoons, four oboes, two trumpets, two horns and drums. In the tune of nineteen were six trebles from the Chapel Royal; the rest of, men, were altos, tenors and basses. Frasi, Galli and Beard drove the five soloists, who were required to help the melody. For this execution the transposed Guadagni arias were reestablished to the soprano voice. By 1754 Handel was seriously harassed by the beginning of visual deficiency, and in 1755 he turned over the heading of the "Savior" clinic execution to his understudy, J.C. Smith. He obviously continued his obligations in 1757 and may have proceeded from that point. The last execution of the work at which Handel was available was at Covent Garden on 6 April 1759, eight days before his passing.
