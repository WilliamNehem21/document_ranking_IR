In 1891 the organization workplaces were moved from Waterloo to another structure in Guild Street with direct access to the station. That year the Great North assumed control over the Palace Hotel (shut after a flame in 1941), close to the joint railroad station in Aberdeen and modernized it, introducing electric lighting and building a secured path between the lodging and station. Energized by its prosperity, the organization acquired consent in 1893 to fabricate an inn and fairway at Cruden Bay, about north of Aberdeen. The lodging was connected to the Great North by the Boddam Branch, another single-track branch from Ellon, on the Buchan area, which served Cruden Bay and angling town at Boddam. The line opened in 1897 with administrations from Ellon taking around forty minutes. The inn opened in 1899, associated with the railroad station by the Cruden Bay Hotel Tramway. This was about long, with a measure of and worked by electric tramcars that took power from an overhead line. Occasional through administrations to Aberdeen started in 1899 with an up administration in the first part of the day; for certain years an evening up administration returned at night. Outings for voyagers had worked on the Deeside Line from 1881, later joined by extraordinary administrations on the Strathspey Line and the Coast Line nearby the Moray Firth, advanced as the "Scottish Riviera".
